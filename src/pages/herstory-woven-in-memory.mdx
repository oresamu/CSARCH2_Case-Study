## The Physical Evolution of Instruction Storage

Rather than just listing historic names and dates, this exhibit digs deep into the structural building blocks of computing
architecture. We explore how early storage technology physically changed over time—evolving from mechanical punch cards to
electromagnetic core memory—and how these early methods directly governed how a processor read and carried out instructions. 

---

## 1. The Timeline of Hidden Pioneers

### Ada Lovelace (1840s) — The Logic of Physical Mediums
  <TextWithImage
  body="Ada Lovelace pioneered the concept of translating abstract mathematical ideas into a sequence of physical instructions that
    a machine could execute. While working on Charles Babbage's proposed Analytical Engine, she expanded her 1843 translation
    of Luigi Menabrea's article with notes that were three times longer than the original. Within these notes, she described
    what is widely regarded as the first published algorithm specifically intended to be processed by a machine, earning her
    recognition as the world's first computer programmer."
  imageSrc={adaLovelace.src}
  imageAlt="Image of Ada Lovelace"
  imageCaption="Ada Lovelace"
  />

  <p> Lovelace also recognized that a computing machine could manipulate
    symbols as well as numbers, allowing it to perform tasks beyond arithmetic—such as composing music or processing other
    forms of information—provided it was given the appropriate instructions. She further argued that while machines could
    faithfully execute programmed commands, they could not independently generate original ideas, foreshadowing modern
    discussions surrounding artificial intelligence. Through her use of punched cards as a method for encoding sequential
    instructions, Lovelace demonstrated that software could exist independently of the machine itself, laying the conceptual
    foundation for programmable, general-purpose computing. </p>

* **Architectural Impact:** Lovelace's work established the fundamental computing paradigm in which software instruction
streams are separated from the underlying execution hardware, a principle that remains central to modern computer
architecture.

### Katherine Johnson (1950s - 1960s) — Human Reference Architectures
  <TextWithImage
  body="Katherine Johnson transformed complex mathematical theory into precise computational procedures that enabled the safe
    execution of early space missions. As one of NASA's renowned &quot;human computers,&quot; she manually calculated and verified
    orbital trajectories, launch windows, and reentry paths for Project Mercury and the Apollo program. Even after NASA
    introduced electronic computers, astronaut John Glenn requested that Johnson personally verify the computer-generated
    calculations before his historic 1962 orbital mission, demonstrating the critical role of human validation in
    computational systems."
  imageSrc={katherineJohnson.src}
  imageAlt="Image of Katherine Johnson"
  imageCaption="Katherine Johnson"
  />
  
  <p> Beyond performing calculations, Johnson developed and refined mathematical methods for
    trajectory analysis, proving that computational accuracy depended not only on machines but also on rigorous
    algorithmic reasoning and verification. Her work established mathematical correctness as a fundamental requirement
    for mission-critical computing and demonstrated the importance of integrating human expertise with automated
    computation, principles that continue to guide the design of reliable and safety-critical computer systems today. </p>

* **Architectural Impact:** When NASA programmed its early IBM mainframes, Johnson's hand-calculated tables were
directly used to verify and audit the integrity of the computer’s memory and CPU arithmetic logic units (ALU).
Additionally, it inspired modern practices such as algorithm verification, fault tolerance, and human oversight
in safety-critical computing.

### Grace Hopper (1950s) — Abstracting the Binary Layer
  <TextWithImage
  body="Grace Hopper revolutionized computer programming by transforming it from a process of writing machine-specific
    instructions into one based on human-readable programming languages. While working on the Harvard Mark I and later
    the UNIVAC, she recognized that programmers should be able to write instructions using English-like commands rather
    than numerical machine code. In 1952, Hopper developed one of the first computer compilers, a program that
    automatically translated high-level instructions into machine language. This innovation became the foundation for
    FLOW-MATIC and later influenced the development of COBOL, one of the first standardized, machine-independent
    programming languages."
  imageSrc={graceHopper.src}
  imageAlt="Image of Grace Hopper"
  imageCaption="Grace Hopper"
  />

  <p> By separating programming logic from the underlying hardware, Hopper made software
    development more efficient, portable, and accessible to a broader range of users. Her work fundamentally changed
    the way software was designed and established many of the principles that continue to underpin modern programming
    languages and compiler technology. </p>

* **Architectural Impact:** Hopper's work introduced the abstraction layer between software and hardware through
compilers and high-level programming languages, enabling hardware-independent software development and establishing
a core principle of modern computer architecture.

### Margaret Hamilton (Late 1960s) — Core Rope Memory & Hardwired Logic
  <TextWithImage
  body=" Margaret Hamilton transformed software from a supporting component into a critical engineering discipline through
    her leadership in developing the onboard flight software for NASA's Apollo Guidance Computer. As director of the
    Software Engineering Division at MIT's Instrumentation Laboratory, she led the team responsible for the software
    that guided astronauts during the Apollo missions. Working under severe memory and processing constraints, Hamilton
    designed software that prioritized essential tasks and could recover gracefully from unexpected conditions. During
    the Apollo 11 lunar landing, these fault-tolerant mechanisms enabled the Guidance Computer to ignore lower-priority
    tasks and continue executing critical landing operations despite receiving overload alarms, allowing the mission
    to proceed safely."
  imageSrc={margaretHamilton.src}
  imageAlt="Image of Margaret Hamilton"
  imageCaption="Margaret Hamilton"
  />

  <p> Hamilton also helped popularize the term software engineering, emphasizing that software
    development should follow the same rigorous principles as other engineering disciplines. Her innovations in
    priority scheduling, asynchronous processing, error recovery, and software reliability laid the foundation for
    modern real-time and mission-critical computing, influencing the design of operating systems and safety-critical
    software that continue to power aerospace, medical, and industrial systems today. </p>

* **Architectural Impact:** Hamilton's work established the principles of fault-tolerant software, priority-based
task scheduling, and real-time system reliability, demonstrating that robust software architecture is essential
for the safe operation of mission-critical computer systems.
